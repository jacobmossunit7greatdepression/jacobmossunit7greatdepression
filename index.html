Jacob Moss | U.S. History Honors | Period 8

Unit Inquiry Statement

The Great Depression and the New Deal highlight how systemic crises demand innovative governance and the balance between individual needs and societal responsibilities.

Related Questions

Factual Question: What were the key causes of the Great Depression?
The Great Depression developed due to several economic weaknesses that collided instead of one single failure, and the stock market crash in 1929 revealed deeper problems. This included banks that were unstable and poorly regulated, factories producing more goods than people could afford, and wealth being undistributed among fewer Americans. As consumer spending dropped, businesses cut jobs, which reduced spending even more. Additionally, the government's slow response allowed these problems to worsen instead of being controlled early.
Conceptual Question: How does government intervention impact economic recovery and social wellbeing?
Government intervention can shape how quickly an economy stabilizes and how protected people feel during a crisis. For example, when governments step in, they can regulate banks, create jobs, and provide relief that prevents a full collapse, however without intervention, fear and instability can spread. At the same time, too much or poorly planned involvement can cause resistance. This shows that public trust plays a big role in whether policies actually work.
Debatable Question: Did the New Deal effectively resolve the challenges of the Great Depression? Why or why not?
The New Deal did not end the Great Depression on its own, but it did change the situation in important ways. As a result, programs focused on relief and reform helped reduce suffering and made everyday life more stable for many Americans and permanently expanded the federal government's responsibility in economic and social issues. Even though full recovery came later, the New Deal shaped how the country moved forward. Honestly, I would say the New Deal was more effective at stabilizing and rebuilding trust than it is sometimes given credit for.
Faith-Related Question: How do Christian principles of stewardship and care for others align with responses to economic crises?
Christian values include caring for others, especially those who are struggling, and during economic crises, these ideas connect to actions. These ideas include sharing resources, protecting the vulnerable, and acting with responsibility rather than selfishness. These values reveal biblical teachings about justice and love, which can align with efforts to help reduce suffering during times like the Great Depression.

Timeline of Major Events

October 29, 1929 - Black Tuesday
The stock market crashed when investors rushed to sell their shares, which wiped out fortunes and shook confidence in the economy. This event made existing economic weaknesses worse and triggered widespread panic.
November 8, 1932 - Franklin D. Roosevelt elected President
Franklin D. Roosevelt was elected as Americans looked for change after years of worsening economic conditions. In his campaign, he promised stronger government involvement in order to address unemployment and financial instability.
March 4, 1933 - Franklin D. Roosevelt inaugurated President
Franklin D. Roosevelt took office during one of the most desperate times of the Great Depression, with banks failing and millions unemployed. During this time, public confidence in leadership was very low.
March 6-13, 1933 - Bank Holiday
The federal government temporarily closed banks nationwide to prevent further collapses. This pause allowed inspections to restore confidence and reopen banks that were financially well.
March 12, 1933 - FDR's first 'Fireside Chat'
Roosevelt addressed Americans over the radio to explain the banking crisis in simple terms. Through this, he helped calm fears and made people feel included during the recovery process.
April 14, 1935 - Black Sunday
A massive dust storm swept across the Great Plains. This made the environmental damage of the Dust Bowl impossible to ignore, and the storm showed how economic and environmental crises were strangely happening at the same time.
August 14, 1935 - Social Security Act signed into Law
The Social Security Act created federal support for the elderly, unemployed, and disabled. This marked a major shift in how the government accepted responsibility for citizens' wellbeing.

Factual Question: What were the main causes of the Dust Bowl?
The Dust Bowl was caused by severe drought on top of poor farming practices, and when farmers removed native grasses to plant crops, it left soil loose. When drought and wind hit, massive dust storms followed.

Human and business decisions can damage the environment, especially when prioritizing short term profit, and government action can either allow harm or help repair it, depending on response.

The disaster could not have been fully prevented, however its effects could have been reduced with better land management. Better earlier conservation could have significantly reduced most of the damage, even using 1930s methods.

Event #4: Leadership in the Midst of Crisis

SECTION 1: Exploring Macro Concepts

Inquiry Statement: Leadership communication during times of crisis can shape societal resilience, foster unity, and redefine the relationship between citizens and their leaders.
Factual Question: Why is it important that leaders address the nation in times of crisis?
Leaders need to address the nation in a crisis because people need a clear message and reassurance when everything feels unstable. When no one official says anything, fear and rumors take over, and that can make the situation worse. A public address can explain what happened, what is known so far, what actions are being taken, and what people should do next, which builds trust. This is because silence can look like the leader is either absolutely clueless or just avoiding responsibility.
Conceptual Question: What does strong leadership communication look like?
Strong leadership communication is clear, honest, and not full of confusing and fancy words. It should not exaggerate or be overly reassuring, but it also should not pretend the crisis is not serious. Good communication stays consistent so that people are not getting mixed messages, and it focuses on what the public actually needs to know. The best ones also sound human, not like reading off a script.
Debatable Question: Should leaders communicate with empathy and care, or as matter-of-fact? Why?
Leaders should communicate with empathy and care and not be practical. If they sound cold and blunt, people feel brushed off. This is especially when lives are being lost or changed. Empathy does not mean being unclear though, as facts are still necessary for people to respond the right way. I believe the best approach is empathy first with clear facts right alongside it, not empathy as an extra at the end.
Faith-Related Question: In what ways does the leadership style of Jesus stand out in contrast to political leaders today?
The leadership of Jesus stands out because it is centered on serving and helping, not looking powerful. He consistently paid attention to people who were suffering or pushed aside, and he treated them like they mattered, rather than ignoring them or treating them like nothing. A lot of political leadership today can feel like it is about image, winning, or protecting status. Jesus is the complete opposite. His style is about being humble, compassionate, and stepping into other people's shoes instead of running away or avoiding their pain.
