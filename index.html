The Great Depression and the New Deal (1933-1939)

Jacob Moss | U.S. History Honors | Period 8

Dust Storm Approaching, 1930s | Source: The Collector

Unit Inquiry Statement

The Great Depression and the New Deal highlight how systemic crises demand innovative governance and the balance between individual needs and societal responsibilities.

Related Questions

Factual Question: What were the key causes of the Great Depression?
The Great Depression was caused by several economic problems coming together, including the 1929 stock market crash, overproduction of goods, weak and poorly regulated banks, unequal distribution of wealth, declining consumer spending, and a delayed government response.
Conceptual Question: How does government intervention impact economic recovery and social wellbeing?
Government intervention affects economic recovery and social wellbeing by stabilizing markets, creating jobs, supporting those most affected, and rebuilding trust during periods of economic crisis.
Debatable Question: Did the New Deal effectively resolve the challenges of the Great Depression? Why or why not?
The New Deal did not completely end the Great Depression, but it reduced widespread suffering, stabilized key parts of the economy, expanded social programs, and reshaped the federal government's role in addressing economic and social problems, helping support long term recovery.
Faith-Related Question: How do Christian principles of stewardship and care for others align with responses to economic crises?
Christian principles of care for others connect to responses during economic crises by encouraging responsible use of resources, compassion for those in need, and shared responsibility, reflecting biblical teachings about justice and love for others.

Timeline of Major Events

October 29, 1929 - Black Tuesday
The stock market crashed as investors rushed to sell their shares, causing massive financial losses and pushing the United States deeper into economic crisis.
November 8, 1932 - Franklin D. Roosevelt elected POTUS
Franklin D. Roosevelt was elected during the worst years of the Great Depression after promising stronger government action to address economic hardship.
March 4, 1933 - Franklin D. Roosevelt inaugurated POTUS
Franklin D. Roosevelt took office as unemployment, bank failures, and economic uncertainty affected millions of Americans.
March 6-13, 1933 - Bank Holiday
A nationwide bank holiday temporarily closed banks to stop panic withdrawals and restore trust in the banking system.
March 12, 1933 - FDR's first 'Fireside Chat'
Franklin D. Roosevelt spoke directly to Americans over the radio, explaining the banking crisis in clear language and reassuring the public.
April 14, 1935 - Black Sunday
A powerful dust storm swept across the Great Plains, showing the severe environmental damage and human suffering caused by the Dust Bowl.
August 14, 1935 - Social Security Act signed into Law
The Social Security Act created federal programs to support the elderly, unemployed, and disabled, marking a major expansion of the government's role during the Great Depression.

Event #1: The Great Depression

SECTION 1: Exploring Macro Concepts

Inquiry Statement: Societal responses to crises, such as the Great Depression, reveal the evolving dynamics of systems and human resilience, reflecting historical lessons applicable to modern economic challenges.
Factual Question: What were the main causes of the Great Depression?
The Great Depression was caused by several economic problems happening at the same time, including the 1929 stock market crash, overproduction of goods, weak banking systems, unequal distribution of wealth, and a major drop in consumer spending. As banks failed and businesses closed, unemployment rose quickly, making the economic crisis even worse.
Conceptual Question: How do economic policies impact global interactions and societies, as well as individuals?
Economic policies affect how societies respond to hardship and how countries interact with one another. Strong and balanced policies can stabilize markets, support cooperation between nations, and protect citizens during crises, while weak policies can lead to instability and global tension. On an individual level, these policies influence job security, income, and access to basic needs.
Debatable Question: Is the impact of the Great Depression a testament to the failure of capitalist economic structures? When might we see the next economic depression?
The Great Depression revealed serious weaknesses in largely unregulated capitalism rather than proving that capitalism itself had failed. The crisis showed the need for regulation and protections to prevent extreme economic collapse. Another major depression could occur if financial systems become unstable again due to poor regulation, excessive risk taking, or major global shocks, though stronger institutions today may lower the chances.

This source is a campaign speech delivered in 1928 by Herbert Hoover during the final stage of his presidential campaign. Hoover was the Republican nominee and former Secretary of Commerce, and the speech reflects his political and economic views before the Great Depression began. The purpose of the speech was to persuade voters to support Hoover's vision of government based on rugged individualism, self-reliance, and limited federal involvement in the economy. Hoover aimed to defend the American economic system and warn against increased government control and socialist ideas. The document argues that American progress is rooted in individual freedom, equal opportunity, and private enterprise. Hoover criticizes the growth of government power during World War I and claims that continued government involvement in business would threaten liberty and self-government. Throughout the speech, he compares American individualism with European state controlled systems and stresses that economic freedom is necessary for national success. This source is valuable because it clearly explains Hoover's economic beliefs just before the Great Depression and helps explain his later resistance to direct federal relief. It provides insight into how many Americans viewed government intervention in the late 1920s and shows the beliefs shaping early responses to economic crisis. The source is limited because it presents only Hoover's perspective as a political candidate and does not reflect the experiences of Americans who later faced widespread poverty and unemployment. Since it was written before the Great Depression, it underestimates how severe the crisis would become and the limits of rugged individualism during a national emergency.

Analysis Questions

1. How might this source have influenced people's opinions, actions, or beliefs when it was released?
When this speech was released, it likely strengthened public support for limited government and personal responsibility. Hoover's focus on freedom and opportunity may have reassured voters that the American economic system was strong and able to continue growing. This message encouraged confidence in private business and voluntary action rather than federal involvement.
2. How does understanding the historical context change your interpretation of this source, or the historical event?
Knowing that this speech was delivered before the Great Depression changes how it is understood. At the time, economic growth made Hoover's arguments seem reasonable and hopeful. Viewed later, the speech shows how existing economic ideas were not prepared for a crisis of such size, helping explain why early responses to the Depression were limited.

The document supports the understanding that early responses to the Great Depression were shaped by strong belief in individualism and limited government. SECTION 5: So What? Why is this relevant today?

The Great Depression is still relevant today because it shows how quickly economic problems can grow when major warning signs are ignored. In the early 1930s, weak banking systems, unregulated financial markets, and unequal wealth distribution combined to create massive unemployment and poverty. As banks failed and businesses closed, everyday life was affected almost immediately. These events show how closely financial systems are tied to people's basic needs and why economic safeguards matter.

The Great Depression also highlights the role government action can play during a crisis. Early responses that avoided strong intervention allowed economic conditions to worsen, while later New Deal programs helped stabilize the economy and restore confidence. Actions such as bank reform, public work programs, and the creation of Social Security helped reduce suffering and support recovery. These examples continue to shape how governments respond to modern economic challenges, including recessions and financial downturns.

Finally, the Great Depression remains important because it reveals the long lasting effects of economic hardship on society. Many families faced hunger, homelessness, and uncertainty even after the economy began to recover. Similar concerns appear today during periods of inflation, job loss, and global instability. Studying the Great Depression helps apply lessons about economic responsibility, fairness, and resilience to current and future challenges.
